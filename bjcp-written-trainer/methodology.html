The Method Behind the Trainer

This trainer was built around my own written exam study process. Instead of memorizing every vital statistic for every style, I focus on a smaller set of anchor points I can apply quickly under time pressure.

The Core Anchors

The process starts with three style characteristics:

  • Relative strength — how strong the beer should be
  • Body / finish — how dry or full the beer should finish
  • Overall balance — whether the beer is malt-forward, balanced, or bitterness-driven

From those anchors, I can estimate the rest of the recipe: original gravity, grist weight, bitterness, mash approach, and fermentation profile.

1. Estimate Original Gravity

Once I know the target ABV and expected finishing gravity, I estimate original gravity using fast mental math.

The Gravity Drill accepts answers within ±1 gravity point, so the goal is not software-level precision. The goal is a reliable estimate that can be done quickly.

The formal relationship is:

ABV = (OG − FG) × 131

Rearranged:

Gravity drop = ABV ÷ 0.131

A common shortcut is:

ABV × 8

This is fast, but it consistently runs high compared to the formal calculation.

ABV ×8 Shortcut Formal ÷0.131 Difference
4.0% 32 30.5 +1.5
5.0% 40 38.2 +1.8
6.0% 48 45.8 +2.2
7.0% 56 53.4 +2.6

Because of that drift, I use a modified shortcut:

ABV × 8, then subtract 2 gravity points

Example: 5.0% ABV × 8 = 40 gravity points. Subtract 2, and the estimated gravity drop is 38 points.

With an expected FG of 1.010, that gives an estimated OG of 1.048.

The formal calculation gives 38.2 points, so the adjusted shortcut is close while staying easy to do mentally.

2. Estimate Grist Weight

Once OG is estimated, I use a simplified metric grist estimate.

This trainer assumes:

  • 21.5 liters into fermenter
  • 70% brewhouse efficiency
  • common base malt yield assumptions

Under those assumptions, the shortcut is:

OG points ÷ 10 ≈ kilograms of grist

Example: 1.048 OG has 48 gravity points, so the estimated grist weight is about 4.8 kg.

3. Estimate Bitterness

This module does not require one specific method for learning IBUs.

I use BU:GU as a practical guide: original gravity gives the size of the beer, and style balance helps determine how much bitterness makes sense for that gravity.

The goal is not to memorize isolated IBU numbers. The goal is to estimate a defensible bitterness range that fits the beer’s strength, finish, and style balance.

4. Select Mash and Fermentation Choices

Once the beer’s structure is established, the mash and fermentation choices should support the target result.

  • A dry beer generally needs a more fermentable wort
  • A fuller beer may need a more body-supporting mash
  • A clean lager needs restrained fermentation
  • An expressive Belgian style needs the right yeast and fermentation profile
